Technologically Complex Rooms

For those of you who are just getting into escape room design. You can sometimes look at the market and go “where do I begin?” and that is what this blog is for. Now, I won’t be giving a step-by-step tutorial on how each of these works. But I will be giving the basic knowledge that hopefully will help you decide on what you need to research.

There are multiple controller types so let’s break them down first.

Firstly, we have micro controllers. These are chip sets that have a central brain (such as the atmega328p) that can run basic I/O operations. The most basic one (and the one I started with) is the Arduino Uno. The Arduino controllers were originally made for teaching in educational settings but have slowly started to span all the way to industrial technologies. These controllers are quite simple. They read incoming voltage signals (mainly high and low inputs) with a couple of ports for analog data (0 to 255). And depending on what they have been programmed to do. Send out high, low, or analog signals based on the information provided. Arduinos can also output serial data to talk to other controllers. Arduinos also come in many form factors. Each with their own pros and cons. You have the Uno, the standard size. The micro removes most of the complex features and going for a compact form factor. As well as the mega with more I/O inputs and outputs. These aren’t all the form factors, but the major ones. Overtime however, as projects become more complex. Micro controllers just won’t cut it anymore. From their low computational power preventing real time readings. To the inability to run complex data sets such as audio and video. You will eventually have to upgrade. And that is where single board computers come in.

What is a single board computer? A single board computer as the name implies is a computer made from a single “board” or PCB (printed circuit board). These computers range in complexity and price but the most common of them all is the Raspberry Pi. Raspberry Pi has been the go-to brand for many. This is mainly due to how opensource the pi is. Although the hardware is not opensource, its software ecosystem and operating system are. This has allowed the community to extensively develop its features. But enough chat about how good the pi is (not sponsored). Why use it over a micro controller? Well for starters, single board computers usually have multiple things that micro controllers don’t. They have dedicated RAM, CPU, and some even have a dedicated graphics unit. This allows for complex data operations. Another great thing about single board computers is the compatibility with off-the-shelf products. With micro controllers, anytime you want to run something on them. You have to make it yourself. From LEDs to running a keyboard. It all must be DIY. With single board computers, most of them run off a basic Linux operating system. This means (almost) anything that you use on your day-to-day computer can run from your single board computer. These are great for running small servers in your escape rooms, or a central control device for less complex rooms. And this is where most escape rooms stop. But we can take this a step further with centralized servers.

What is a centralized server? A centralized server is used for heavy computational power that is beyond the power of any single board computer. This can span from running video game engines (such as unity) to control your game. All the way to audio servers. The main use case for hobbyists however is show control. With single board computers you can run pre-rendered videos. These are your mp4 files and such. But with servers you can run videos that change based on your game state. Imagine you have a submarine room. In this room you want your players after they solve a puzzle to be able to “dive” into the sea. You would have your dedicated server running a live software renderer that will loop an “above sea” video until the players “dive” where it would then be able to render the “dive” live in action. This usually is synchronized with lighting effects as well through some sort of DMX control (will be discussed in a later blog). Show control can really immerse your players, with its only downside being the cost and complexity.

And this is where your hobbyist normally stops. There are some notable controllers, however, that I did not mention here that do need some recognition. Those being the PLCs (programmable logic controllers), lighting controllers (dedicated hardware), audio controllers (dedicated hardware), and prop controllers.

For the PLCs, lighting, and audio controllers. I left them out because they all require specialized knowledge and can be a conversation for another blog post. They are great for each use case but at the price of the units, and the knowledge required to run them. I left them out of this initial blog. When it comes to prop controllers, in my opinion they are overpriced for something that could be made cheaper. If you are not technically inclined or just need a quick solution, they are perfect. But at the price that most companies sell them for when you can just make your own, why not?

Anyways, that is all for this blog. Work hard, stay creative, stay curious.
