Design Process of Escape Rooms

The design process of an escape room is one of the most important topics when it comes to making an escape room. It's what will make or break your escape room and the flow of it. Understanding the design process will not only save you time and money but will take your room design from mediocre to amazing and is one of the many skills you must master before attempting to make your first room.

So, what is the design process?


(Image Source)

The design process is a process that gives a proper workflow to design anything from problem to solution. There are many different design process workflows but today I'll be going over the one that works best for me. This process may not be the best for you, some people may want more or less steps, and that's okay. But having a process in the long run will save you time, money, and brain power. The design process I use has the following steps.

- Ask

- Research

- Imagine

- Plan

- Create

- Test

- Improve

Each step moves closer to your end product. What does each step mean though? Let's dive deeper into each step.

In the first step, ask, we “Identify the need and constraints”. (Engineering Design Process - TeachEngineering) In escape room design this is whatever your main objective is. You should never go into a room design with just a genre or theme, you need to have an objective. Whether this is a story line, a technological idea you want to have be your room's centerpiece, or even as simple as the room environment. You need to have an idea you can design around. Also, if you are a designer working for a customer, this is where you need to find out what the constraints on the project are. From financial, to installation. What is the customers vision.

In the research step we research our project, this is the phase you will spend most of your time on. This is where you research your idea, this can be in a range of mood boards, reading literature, watching videos, and in the case of design, researching if what you are doing is possible, this step needs to be extremely thorough as not researching enough can add delays in your project.

In this step, we generate ideas to solve our solution. To do this, take your ideas you generated in the research step, and delve deeply into them seeing if it's possible to create what you see in your head. This can be from finding out if you should use spray foam to make your rock formations, or hand carve them. Whether using LED light strips is best for you, or DMX lights. Find out what approach you want to take, as none are universal, and each one has pros and cons.

Take all the research you have gathered from the last two steps and ask yourself, which of these solutions is the best for MY PROJECT . And I emphasize the importance of “My Project”. Many times, I have had someone tell me that their solution is better. And it could be, but when creating something do what is best for you. Whether it is cheaper, you're more comfortable with said solution, or you want to learn something new. There is no correct answer in escape room design as in the end its personal preference. For example, in escape room automation, some companies have chosen to use PLCs (Programmable Logic Controllers) as they know how to program them, even though they can be pricey. Some companies choose to use a server rack (such as what I use in my Nuclear Codes: Save The Nation escape room box (raspberry pi 4)) since they are cheaper and there is more openly available knowledge about them. And some companies might not automate anything and leave it to the game guide running the room. All are great options, each with their own pros and cons.

I like to call this phase the prototyping phase instead of the create phase, as this is where you will make your first prototype. Take every bit of information you got from the last steps and put it into a functioning prototype. This can be from your puzzles, to set design, to your tech in the room. Everything needs to be prototyped. DO NOT waste your time and money making a final product, as most of the time, the first iteration will not be what you desire.

Test what you have, when it comes to this phase this will be the most important of them all when it comes to your game flow and puzzle design. Get people to play test your games, evaluate their gameplay, and ask them questions. I will go more into this step in a later blog post, but I like to do the following when playtesting.

1. Observe - Observe the behavior of your players, how are they reacting to the environment around them. What are they doing you weren't expecting.

2. Notate - Take notes, always bring a pen and paper or something equivalent to your play tests. Take notes of things the players do wrong, what they do right, what you can improve on, and what needs fixed.

3. Ask - When the players are done ask them questions, what did they like, what did they dislike. At the end of the day, your puzzle might be perfect, but if no one likes it then why should you use it.

Improve : This step will never be finished, you can get what you want, but you will never finish any room. You will always think it needs something new, or something needs to be done better. At the end of the day nothing is perfect. But we can always do our best to improve our end product. Take your tests, improve the room based on them, and repeat.

Another thing is the design process is not linear. You will go back and forth between each step multiple times, and that's not wrong at all. Another thing is that the design process takes time, don't expect it to happen overnight, it can take days, weeks, months, even years to get to your final product. And even then, something will always need to be improved upon.

If there is anything you take back from this, I want it to be this. Have fun when creating something, do stuff you are comfortable with, and don't be afraid to try new things. We design rooms not because of the financial income they bring (though that is a plus), but we do this because we enjoy designing.

Work hard, stay creative, stay curious.
